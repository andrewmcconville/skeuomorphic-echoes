# p2.1: Skeuomorph Selection

## 1. Personal Goals

**What do you hope to make?**
A museum kiosk interface that serves as both wayfinding guide and collectible-driven exploration tool, greeting visitors at each exhibit entrance and inviting them to collect evolutionary stages as they explore.

**Why does this project matter to you?**
AI prompting is replacing the toolbars, rulers, and drop-down menus of traditional editors. If natural-language interaction removes the need for visual metaphors altogether, skeuomorphs may become artifacts themselves.

---

## 2. Content & Experience Format

**Primary Skeuomorph:**
The WYSIWYG page editor, tracing from Google Docs cloud collaboration → Adobe InDesign → X-Acto knife paste-ups → Linotype machines → movable type → hand-written manuscripts.

**Why this skeuomorph?**
The WYSIWYG editor is the most popular and taken-for-granted skeuomorph in daily digital life where virtual pages mimic paper, clipboards reference paste-up boards, and font menus appear as mini type specimen books.

**Three secondary skeuomorphs:**
1. **Camera shutter → Shutter sound effect.** Phones play a mechanical click when taking a photo, and even when capturing a screenshot, where no camera is involved at all.
2. **Envelope → Email icon.** The sealed paper envelope persists as the symbol for electronic messages decades after physical mail stopped being the default.
3. **Hanging up the phone → End Call button.** Replacing a handset on its cradle became a red button tap, preserving the gesture of a rotary-era device most users have never touched.

**Why these skeuomorphs?**
Each preserves a physical object (a shutter, an envelope, a handset) as a sound, icon, or gesture in software that no longer needs the original thing to function.

**UX Pattern:**
Museum kiosk with wayfinding and collecting.

**Why this format?**
Each kiosk orients visitors to the room they're entering while offering collectible evolutionary stages, turning a passive gallery walk into an active scavenger hunt through design history.

**Media types:**
Typographic: the content is mostly written information. Supported by historical photography, diagrams of tool-icon lineages, and wayfinding floor-plan maps.

**Why this media approach?**
Since the primary skeuomorph is typography, the letterforms themselves carry the narrative, while the wayfinding layer uses clean iconography and floor-plan visuals to orient visitors spatially.

## 3. Research Sources

**Informational content sources:**
Bringhurst's *The Elements of Typographic Style*, the Letterform Archive's online collection, and the Computer History Museum's desktop publishing archives.

**Visual content sources:**
The Letterform Archive's digitized type specimens, the Computer History Museum's early DTP screenshots, and archive.org for scanned specimen books and software documentation.

**Why are these sources appropriate?**
Each provides verifiable primary-source material. Bringhurst is the canonical typographic reference, the Letterform Archive holds over 100,000 original design objects, and the Computer History Museum documented the DTP revolution firsthand.

## 4. Inspiration Sites

**Site 1: [Klim Type Foundry](https://klim.co.nz/)**
Swiss-inspired grids, interactive type testers, and an energy that makes design history feel like a living workshop.

**Site 2: [Histography.io](https://histography.io/)**
Progressive-disclosure timeline that zooms from a full historical sweep down to individual events. This is the navigation metaphor I want for exploring typographic eras at each kiosk.

**Site 3: [Letterform Archive Online Archive](https://oa.letterformarchive.org/)**
Browsable, filterable collection of 3,500+ digitized design artifacts with search by decade, country, and format. The curatorial depth and serendipitous discovery model I want for my evolutionary stage content.

## 5. Form Factor

**Viewport:**
1440×2560 px, portrait orientation, wall-mounted touchscreen.

**Why?**
Portrait mirrors the vertical proportions of a physical page, the central artifact of the WYSIWYG skeuomorph. The fixed resolution allows precise positioning like a typeset museum placard.

**Format:**
Web app.

**Why?**
The kiosk needs room-specific content, collectible tracking, touch navigation without browser chrome, and inactivity resets, all requiring app-level state management.

**Input mode:**
Coarse (finger/touch).

**Why?**
Museum visitors expect to walk up and interact immediately with generously sized touch targets, echoing the tactile history of compositors physically handling type.

**Location/context of use:**
Museum kiosk at the entrance to each of four exhibit rooms on a single floor.

**Why?**
Each kiosk must function both as a quick 30-second orientation and a rich 5-minute deep-dive depending on visitor intent.
