Overview

Each example begins with a specific skeuomorphic artifact — an icon, a sound, a gesture, a word, or a visual metaphor — and traces its evolution backward to a physical origin.

 

Example Skeuomorphs

  1. The QWERTY Keyboard Layout
    1. Onscreen smartphone keyboard
    2. Physical computer keyboard
    3. Electric typewriter
    4. Typewriter with mechanical arms
    5. Handwritten letter

    The onscreen keyboard preserves the QWERTY layout designed in 1873 to prevent mechanical typewriter arms from jamming — a physical constraint that hasn't existed for over a century.

  2. The Shutter Click Sound
    1. Screenshot capture
    2. Smartphone camera
    3. Digital SLR camera
    4. Large-format bellows camera
    5. Camera obscura tracing

    Phones play a fake mechanical shutter sound mimicking the SLR mirror slap, even though no moving parts exist — even when capturing a screenshot, which involves no optics at all.

  3. The 👍 "Like" Button
    1. Heart icons, upvotes, and emoji reactions across apps
    2. Facebook's thumbs-up "Like" button
    3. Stamping a document "Approved"
    4. Raising a hand to vote "aye" in a public assembly
    5. Roman crowd gesturing thumbs up or down in the arena

    The thumbs-up "like" icon references an ancient physical gesture of approval — a raised thumb in a Roman amphitheater — now reduced to a single tap that carries no bodily gesture at all.

  4. The Spinning Disc and Album Art
    1. Streaming service spinning-disc animation
    2. Compact disc
    3. Compact cassette
    4. Vinyl record on a turntable
    5. Phonograph cylinder
    6. Live singing performance

    Music apps display spinning album art or a rotating disc animation mimicking the physical spin of vinyl and CDs. Spotify's interface still organizes music into "albums" — a word for a bound collection of sleeves that held records.

  5. The Gold Coin Icon for Digital Currency
    1. Bitcoin and cryptocurrency
    2. Online banking transfer
    3. Plastic credit card swipe
    4. Paper banknotes backed by gold
    5. Stamped gold coins with sovereign marks
    6. Gold nuggets traded by weight

    Bitcoin is universally represented as a shiny gold coin despite being nothing more than a cryptographic entry on a distributed ledger. The "₿" symbol is stylized like a stamped metal coin.

  6. The "Ding-Dong" Doorbell Chime
    1. Smart doorbell app notification
    2. Wired intercom buzzer
    3. Push-button electric doorbell
    4. String-activated mechanical bell
    5. Knocking on a door with knuckles

    Ring and other smart doorbells send a digital "ding-dong" tone to your phone — mimicking the two-tone electromagnetic chime of a 1930s doorbell, triggered by someone standing at a door you may be miles away from.

  7. The Word "Mail" in Email
    1. Encrypted instant message
    2. Electronic mail (email)
    3. Telegraph message
    4. Organized postal mail service
    5. Drum signals between villages

    We still call it "mail," use envelope icons, say we "send" and "deliver" messages, and call unwanted messages "junk mail" — all referencing a physical postal system that email has nothing to do with.

  8. The Floppy Disk "Save" Icon
    1. Cloud save button
    2. File saved to hard drive
    3. File saved to floppy disk
    4. Punch card data storage
    5. Handwritten manuscript on parchment
    6. Chiseled stone tablet

    The floppy disk icon remains the universal symbol for "save" despite the object being extinct for decades. Most users have never touched one.

  9. The Envelope Icon for Email
    1. Email inbox
    2. Fax cover sheet
    3. Interoffice memo in a routed envelope
    4. Stamped postal envelope
    5. Wax-sealed scroll

    The envelope icon represents email despite no physical envelope, seal, stamp, or postmark being involved.

  10. The Trash Can on Your Desktop
    1. Desktop Recycle Bin / Trash icon
    2. Paper shredder
    3. Wastebasket
    4. Burn pile

    Deleting a digital file uses a trash can icon, sometimes with a crumpling paper animation and sound effect, referencing a physical receptacle that doesn't exist.

  11. The Manila Folder Icon
    1. Desktop "folder" icon
    2. Manila folder in a filing cabinet
    3. Paper bundles tied with string
    4. Scrolls stored in cubbyholes

    Digital file organization uses the tabbed manila folder shape, a form factor from the 1940s office supply catalog.

  12. The Shopping Cart Icon
    1. Online "Add to Cart" button
    2. Mail-order catalog phone line
    3. Supermarket metal shopping cart
    4. General store counter and ledger
    5. Barter at a market stall

    The shopping cart icon persists in e-commerce even though nothing physical is being wheeled anywhere.

  13. The Phone Handset Icon
    1. Smartphone "call" button
    2. Cordless home phone
    3. Desk handset telephone
    4. Candlestick telephone
    5. Speaking tube
    6. Shouting across a field

    The icon for making a call is still the silhouette of a curved handset receiver — an object most users under 25 have never held to their ear.

  14. The Page Turn Animation
    1. E-reader page curl animation
    2. PDF document
    3. Newspaper broadsheet
    4. Codex with bound pages
    5. Papyrus scroll unrolling

    E-readers simulate a curling paper corner and a swishing sound when you "turn" a page that doesn't physically exist.

  15. The Magnifying Glass for Search
    1. Search bar magnifying glass icon
    2. Ctrl+F find-in-document
    3. Handheld magnifying glass
    4. Polished reading stone
    5. Scanning with the naked eye

    Every search bar uses a magnifying glass icon despite digital search involving no optical magnification whatsoever.

  16. The Paperclip for Attachments
    1. Email attachment paperclip icon
    2. Bent-wire paperclip
    3. Ribbon-tied legal documents
    4. Straight pin through papers
    5. Wax seal holding pages together

    Attaching a digital file to an email is represented by a paperclip — a 19th-century fastener for loose sheets of paper.

  17. "CC" — Carbon Copy
    1. Email CC field
    2. Photocopier
    3. Carbon paper between typewritten sheets
    4. Letter press duplication
    5. Scribe copying by hand

    The CC field in every email client stands for "carbon copy," referencing the messy blue carbon sheets typists placed between pages.

  18. The Typewriter Keyclick on a Phone Keyboard
    1. Smartphone haptic key-click sound
    2. Mechanical keyboard switches
    3. Electric typewriter keystroke
    4. Typewriter key striking an inked ribbon
    5. Quill scratching on parchment

    Phone keyboards play an artificial click and deliver a small vibration mimicking a typewriter keystroke that never happens.

  19. The Bell Icon for Notifications
    1. App notification bell icon
    2. Electric buzzer
    3. Telephone ringer
    4. Mechanical school bell
    5. Servant call bell on a front desk
    6. Town crier ringing a hand bell

    The bell-shaped icon represents push notifications despite no physical bell, clapper, or ringing mechanism being involved.

  20. "Hanging Up" the Phone
    1. Tapping the red "hang up" button
    2. Placing a rotary handset on the cradle
    3. Hanging a candlestick receiver on its hook
    4. Replacing a speaking-tube earpiece
    5. Walking away from a conversation

    We say "hang up" and tap an icon of a handset dropping downward, despite there being nothing to physically hang on anything.

  21. "Dialing" a Phone Number
    1. Telling a voice assistant to "dial" someone
    2. Tapping a contact on a glass screen
    3. Pressing touch-tone buttons
    4. Rotating a rotary dial
    5. Requesting a switchboard operator by number
    6. Calling out someone's name

    We still say "dial a number" decades after the rotary dial vanished from everyday life.

  22. The Volume Knob Slider
    1. On-screen volume slider
    2. Radio volume knob
    3. Gramophone horn and needle placement
    4. Pipe organ bellows and stops
    5. Singing louder or softer

    The on-screen slider or arc mimics the rotational gesture of turning a physical knob that isn't there.

  23. The Toggle Switch
    1. iOS-style on/off toggle in settings
    2. Electrical toggle switch
    3. Mechanical gate latch
    4. Wooden lever

    The rounded UI toggle slides left and right to mimic flipping a physical switch, complete with a color change from gray to green.

  24. The Desktop Metaphor Itself
    1. Virtual OS workspace with taskbar and dock
    2. First computer "desktop" with icons
    3. Office desk with paper trays and in/out boxes
    4. Workbench surface

    The entire computer interface is a skeuomorph — files, folders, a trash can, and a "desktop" all mimic a physical office that no longer needs to exist.

  25. The Wallet
    1. Apple Wallet / Google Wallet
    2. Checkbook in a cover
    3. Leather billfold
    4. Drawstring coin pouch

    Digital wallets display your cards fanned out as if tucked into leather card slots, complete with embossed textures and raised lettering on the card images.

  26. The Loading Hourglass / Spinner
    1. Spinning loading wheel
    2. Hourglass cursor icon
    3. Blinking command-line cursor
    4. Watching a clock's second hand
    5. Watching sand in an hourglass
    6. Watching a sundial's shadow creep

    The "please wait" indicator evolved from a literal hourglass icon to a spinning wheel, both referencing the passage of idle time through a physical object.

  27. The Bookmark Ribbon
    1. Browser "bookmark"
    2. Dog-eared page corner
    3. Leather bookmark strap
    4. Ribbon sewn into a scroll

    The bookmark icon in browsers and apps is often a ribbon shape — a reference to the cloth strip bound into the spine of a physical book.

  28. "Burning" a Disc
    1. Dragging files to a disc icon with a flame graphic
    2. "Burning" a CD with a laser
    3. Stamping a vinyl record from a master
    4. Pressing a wax seal into paper
    5. Branding livestock with a hot iron

    We said "burn a CD" because the laser scorched pits into the disc, and the language and fire iconography persisted long after the reason.

  29. "Rolling Down" the Window
    1. Telling a voice assistant to "roll down the window"
    2. Pressing an electric car window button
    3. Turning a hand-crank car window
    4. Lifting a sash window by hand
    5. Pushing open a wooden shutter

    We say "roll down" referencing a hand-crank mechanism that hasn't been standard in cars for decades.

  30. The Lock Icon for Security
    1. HTTPS lock icon in the browser bar
    2. Biometric fingerprint scanner
    3. Electronic keycard reader
    4. Combination dial lock
    5. Iron padlock and key
    6. Wooden bar across a door

    The tiny padlock next to a URL represents encrypted data transmission using the image of a medieval fastening device.

  31. The Clipboard for Copy/Paste
    1. Copy/paste with a clipboard icon
    2. "Clipboard" in computer memory
    3. Holding a sheet on a spring-clip clipboard
    4. Copying onto a wax tablet
    5. Scribe memorizing and rewriting text

    The system that temporarily holds copied text is called a "clipboard" after the hinged board that holds paper in place with a metal clip.

  32. "Tuning In"
    1. "Tune in to our livestream"
    2. Clicking a TV remote
    3. Selecting a TV channel with a knob
    4. Turning a radio frequency dial
    5. Adjusting a crystal radio set
    6. Cupping a hand to your ear

    We say "tune in" to a podcast or livestream despite there being no frequency to calibrate or antenna to adjust.

  33. "Rewinding"
    1. Tapping the "rewind 10 seconds" button on a streaming app
    2. Rewinding a cassette with a pencil
    3. Rewinding magnetic tape on a reel-to-reel
    4. Flipping back through printed pages
    5. Rolling a scroll backward by hand

    The rewind icon with two left-facing triangles references spooling tape backward on reels that no longer exist.

  34. The "Channel"
    1. Slack channel or YouTube channel
    2. TV channel selected by dial
    3. Radio frequency channel
    4. Telegraph communication channel
    5. River channel dug by hand

    The word "channel" for a communication stream traces back to a physical waterway carved to direct flow in one direction.

  35. The "Dashboard"
    1. Software analytics dashboard
    2. Cockpit flight gauges
    3. Car instrument panel
    4. Wooden board on a horse carriage to block splashing mud

    The word "dashboard" originated as a literal board that "dashed" away mud from horses' hooves, now meaning any at-a-glance data display.

  36. The Gear Icon for Settings
    1. Gear/cog icon for app settings
    2. Mechanical watch movement
    3. Water mill gears
    4. Hand-adjusted wooden mechanism

    The gear icon in every app references interlocking metal cogs — a metaphor for adjusting the inner workings of a machine that has no gears.

  37. "Film" and the Film Strip Icon
    1. Streaming video app with a film-strip clapperboard icon
    2. DVD disc
    3. VHS tape
    4. Motion picture film reel
    5. Zoetrope spinning drum
    6. Live stage theater

    Video apps use film-strip frames, clapperboard icons, and the word "film" despite video being entirely digital with no celluloid involved.

  38. "Scrolling"
    1. Swiping a finger to "scroll" a phone screen
    2. Spinning a scroll wheel on a mouse
    3. Cranking a microfilm reader
    4. Unrolling a papyrus scroll by hand

    We say "scroll down" and drag a "scrollbar" — both referencing the physical act of unrolling a wound document between two rods. Nobody is unrolling anything.

  39. The Scissors Icon for "Cut"
    1. "Cut" command in a word processor with a scissors icon
    2. Razor-blading and pasting strips onto a layout board
    3. Cutting paper with scissors
    4. Tearing papyrus by hand

    Cut and paste originated from literally cutting paper with scissors and gluing it onto a new page. The scissors icon persists in every editing toolbar despite nothing being severed.

  40. The Sticky Note
    1. Digital whiteboard sticky notes in Miro or FigJam
    2. Sticking Post-it notes around a computer monitor
    3. Pinning a scrap of paper to a corkboard
    4. Chalking a reminder on a slate

    Digital sticky note apps mimic the yellow square, drop shadow, slightly tilted angle, and handwriting-style font of a physical Post-it — an adhesive-backed paper product that exists nowhere on screen.

  41. "Booting Up" a Computer
    1. Saying "reboot" to a voice assistant
    2. Pressing the power button and watching a loading screen
    3. Loading a program from a paper tape or punch card
    4. Flipping rows of toggle switches on a mainframe
    5. Hand-cranking a machine to start it

    The word "boot" comes from "bootstrapping" — the paradox of a machine loading its own instructions from nothing. We still say "boot up" and "reboot" as if pulling on invisible straps.

  42. The Calendar Icon
    1. Digital calendar app icon with a red binding ring and date number
    2. Tear-off daily calendar pad
    3. Printed wall calendar
    4. Carved stone calendar
    5. Tracking lunar cycles with tally marks on bone

    Calendar app icons display a tiny paper page with a red spiral binding at the top, torn-edge pages, and a bold date number — mimicking a physical tear-off desk calendar that no one tears.

  43. The Inbox / Outbox Trays
    1. Email "Inbox" and "Outbox" labels
    2. Stacking wire desk trays labeled "In" and "Out"
    3. Sorting letters into postal cubbyholes
    4. Receiving a hand-delivered message on a tray

    Every email client organizes messages into an "Inbox" and "Outbox" — referencing the stacking metal wire trays on a physical desk where paper was sorted for action or delivery.

  44. "Rolling" the Credits
    1. Tapping to speed up "rolling" credits on a streaming app
    2. Digital text scrolling upward on a screen
    3. Credits painted on a roller-fed drum scrolling past a lens
    4. Printed title cards held in front of a camera
    5. A herald reading names from a scroll

    End credits "roll" upward on screen, and we say "roll credits" — mimicking a physical drum or scroll that fed text past a camera lens. The text is now just pixels translating vertically.

  45. The Compass Icon for Navigation
    1. Map app compass needle icon
    2. Handheld GPS unit
    3. Nautical compass rose on a printed chart
    4. Magnetic compass with a needle
    5. Navigating by stars and landmarks

    Navigation apps use a compass needle icon to orient the map, despite GPS relying on satellite triangulation — not magnetic north or a spinning needle.

  46. The Light Bulb for Ideas / Tips
    1. 💡 icon for hints, tips, and "bright ideas" in apps
    2. LED bulb
    3. Edison's incandescent light bulb
    4. Gas lamp illumination
    5. Torch or candle flame as a symbol of knowledge

    The light bulb icon for inspiration and tips references Edison's 1879 incandescent design — a glass globe with a visible filament — even though most of the world now lights rooms with LEDs that look nothing like it.

  47. "The Cloud"
    1. Storing files "in the cloud" represented by a fluffy cumulus icon
    2. Renting rack space in a colocation data center
    3. Hosting files on a dedicated server in a closet
    4. Storing records in a physical vault

    Cloud computing is represented as a soft, floating cumulus cloud, as if your data drifts in the sky — rather than sitting on racks in a massive, air-conditioned warehouse drawing megawatts of power.

  48. The Microphone Icon
    1. Voice assistant mic icon shaped like a studio condenser
    2. Tiny MEMS microphone chip in a phone
    3. Handheld dynamic stage microphone
    4. Carbon button microphone
    5. Megaphone or speaking trumpet
    6. Cupping hands around the mouth to project

    Voice search and assistant buttons display a classic studio microphone silhouette — a large, rounded capsule on a stand — despite your phone's actual microphone being a pinhole-sized MEMS chip invisible to the naked eye.

  49. "Debugging" / The Bug Icon
    1. Clicking a beetle icon to report a bug in an app
    2. Running a software debugger tool
    3. Grace Hopper's team taping a real moth into a logbook in 1947
    4. Checking vacuum tubes for burnt-out filaments
    5. Troubleshooting a jammed mechanical loom

    We say "bug" and "debug" and use a beetle icon for error reporting — tracing back to a literal moth that shorted a relay in a Mark II computer. No insects are involved in modern software failures.

  50. The Stamp / Sticker Metaphor
    1. Tapping to place a digital "sticker" in a chat or on a Snapchat photo
    2. Licking and sticking a postage stamp
    3. Inking a rubber stamp and pressing it to paper
    4. Pressing a signet ring into hot wax

    Apps let you "stamp" approval or place "stickers" on photos and messages — referencing physical rubber ink stamps and adhesive-backed paper decals that exist nowhere in a digital image.

  51. "Broadcasting"
    1. Clicking "Go Live" to broadcast on Twitch or YouTube
    2. Airing a television broadcast
    3. Transmitting a radio signal from a tower
    4. A town crier announcing news in the square
    5. A farmer scattering seeds broadly across a field by hand

    The word "broadcast" originally meant casting seeds broadly across plowed soil. It became a radio term for wide transmission, and we still say "broadcast" when streaming to an audience over the internet — where nothing is scattered or transmitted through the air.

  52. "Tablet"
    1. Tapping and drawing on an iPad with a stylus
    2. Using a pen-based PDA like the Palm Pilot
    3. Carrying a paper notepad or legal pad
    4. Scratching notes onto a wax tablet with a metal stylus
    5. Pressing a reed stylus into a wet clay tablet in Mesopotamia

    The word "tablet" for an iPad-like device traces directly back to the clay and wax writing slabs of ancient Mesopotamia — a flat, handheld surface you inscribe with a pointed stick. The form factor, the gesture, and the name have survived five thousand years.
